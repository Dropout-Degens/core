# Positioning Session — Strategies Discussed — February 27, 2026

*These are the betting strategies Dax articulated for DD's educational content. Formatted and cleaned up from the session.*

---

## 1. Bankroll & Unit Sizing

Set a unit size of 1–3% of your total bankroll. Never wager more than 3–5 units in a single day.

This constraint does two things: it limits your downside on any bad day, and it forces you to be selective. When you can only place 3–5 bets, you stop taking bets you aren't truly confident in. You stop filling up a card just to fill it.

A 0.25 unit bet is a valid bet. If your unit size is too large for a given play, scale down — don't skip it or round up.

---

## 2. Time Value of Research

Every bet you place is backed by the research you put into it. More research time = more confidence = better decision. Less research time = less confidence = weaker decision.

Think of it this way: if you spend two hours researching two plays and feel 70% confident in both, those are good bets. If you have 30 minutes left before a game and throw a third bet on with minimal research — is that really the best use of your next unit?

You have a limited number of units to deploy each day. Deploy them where your research is deepest.

There will always be more games tomorrow. Don't force a bet tonight because you're afraid of missing it.

---

## 3. Diminishing Returns Per Bet

Your best bet of the day is your first one — the one you've researched the most and feel strongest about. Every bet after that has a slightly lower expected edge than the one before it.

If you win your first bet of the day, you're at 100%. A second bet, even a good one, risks that floor. If you lose it, you're at 50%. Win the third and you're at 66%. The math works out fine over the long run, but the principle is: don't add bets just because you can. Add bets because they clear the bar you've set for your best work.

The 3–5 unit daily cap mechanically enforces this. It's not a penalty — it's a feature.

---

## 4. Variance

Variance is always real. Even a 90% play loses 1 in 10 times. Even a 60% play goes on cold streaks. You cannot eliminate variance — you can only protect yourself from it destroying your bankroll when it hits.

**The coin analogy:** Imagine a coin that lands heads 90% of the time. You can bet as much as you want, as many times as you want. How much do you put on the first flip? Even at 90%, you account for the 10%. Always.

This is why:
- Unit sizing stays consistent — never raise your unit size during a hot streak, and never chase during a cold one
- You're in this long-term — a bad string of variance at the beginning of a new unit size is dangerous; give any new approach at least 10+ markets before drawing conclusions
- You avoid heavy favorites (e.g., -500 lines) — yes, it's likely to hit, but is risking $500 to win $100 worth the one time it doesn't?

---

## 5. Line Shopping & Live Betting

The line at game time is not always the best line available. Odds move — and you can use that movement to get better prices on plays you already believe in.

**Example:** The Carolina Hurricanes are -200 favorites against the Blackhawks. You think the Hurricanes win. Instead of taking -200 now, you wait. The Blackhawks score first — the line shifts. Suddenly the Hurricanes are +100 or close to even money. You take it. Your expected outcome is the same (the Hurricanes still win the game), but now you're getting twice the return for potentially half the wager.

This applies to spreads too. If you want the Bucks at -2.5 but they open at -8.5, wait. If they go on a run and the live line reaches your number, take it. If it never gets there — you saved your unit for a better spot.

Think of it like shoe reselling: shoes drop at $200 because demand is high. Wait a week and they might be $150. You buy lower and sell higher. The asset is the same — the price is different.

> The goal is always: maximize the return on every unit you wager. Price matters as much as the pick.

---

## 6. Straights Over Parlays

Parlays chain variance. Each leg you add multiplies the ways you can lose.

If you have two plays you feel great about — both at 90% — the combined probability of hitting a 2-leg parlay is 81%. But if both lose individually even 10% of the time, you're compounding those misses together. And the payout almost never compensates for that compounded risk fairly.

More practically: when you parlay, you're essentially betting all of your winnings from the first leg onto the second leg automatically. You lose control of how you size each position.

**The exception — DFS apps (PrizePicks, Underdog, etc.):** These platforms require at least a 2-man entry. The difference is they price their 2-mans at roughly +100, which is a fair price for the chained risk. You're not fighting the vig the same way. But even on DFS apps: don't add legs beyond what the platform requires. Every leg you add increases the ways variance can beat you.

## 7. Implied Probability vs. Your Assessment

Every bet is a comparison between two numbers:
- **The market's implied probability** — what the line suggests the chances are (e.g., -130 = ~56.5% implied)
- **Your assessed probability** — what your research tells you the actual chances are

If your research says a play hits 65% of the time, and the market has it at 50% (even money), that's a strong edge. That's EV. That's the bet you take.

This is the core of everything DD does. The EV alerts from OddsJam identify these gaps at scale. The education layer teaches members how to recognize and trust these gaps on their own.

> The goal is never "I think this team wins." The goal is "I think this team wins at a higher rate than the market implies — and here's why."
